DTI Consumer Protection and Advocacy Bureau
Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

  • Warranty

    • + What is the scope of my warranty?

      The Consumer Act of the Philippines (RA7394) provides consumers the right to seek Repair, Replacement, and Refund as relief in case a consumer product is found to be defective or has an imperfection. In case of Repair, work must conform to the express warranty within 30 days which may be extended by agreement, while Replacement may be elected when the product continues to have a defect or malfunction after a reasonable number of attempts to remedy the problem. If Replacement is not possible, a Refund is allowed but the amount attributable to the use of the consumer prior to the discovery of non-conformity shall be deducted. These remedies are available at no cost to you as the consumer.

      If the product is covered by an express warranty review the terms and conditions provided therein so you may be guided. Should there be no express warranty, the seller, distributor, and/or manufacturer is responsible for implied warranty against defects which shall endure for not less than sixty (60) days nor more than one year following the sale of new consumer products.

      Should there be no action on the part of the establishment and you wish to pursue a complaint, you may file the same by registering at and providing the required details on our consumer complaints website at https://consumercare.dti.gov.ph/#/home

    • + What are express and implied warranty?

      Express warranties are written promises by the seller to the quality of the product. These express warranties specify what the seller’s obligations are in case the product fails and how long a warranty is valid for. If no warranty card or certificate was handed to you upon purchase, then there is an implied warranty that protects your rights as a consumer to seek repair, replacement, and refund within sixty (60) days to one (1) year. In this case, the receipt acts as the warranty card.

    • + Secondhand Products

      Generally, there is no implied warranty in the sale of secondhand articles. Second-hand products do not carry any guarantee and cannot be returned or replaced if they turn out to be defective. The seller or vendor is only liable in the sale of a secondhand product if the seller provides an express written warranty to warrant the general fitness or quality of the product sold to the buyer. Thus, we advise that you revisit the terms and conditions of your contract and check if there is any express written warranty provided therein.

    • + Can we exchange a defective product that we purchased?

      According to the Consumer Act, consumers have the right to have the product repaired, replaced, or refunded if the purchased product is defective or damaged. Also, Repairs must be made within 30 days, but this can be extended if agreed upon.

    • + How do I know if it is still within the warranty period?

      We can check the warranty card. If there is no express warranty, in other words, no written warranty, no warranty card, there is an implied warranty against hidden defects. This will be no less than 60 days and no more than one year.

    • + What if the seller cannot replace the defective product?

      If the seller cannot replace the product, the seller must refund the consumer's payment but must deduct the amount equivalent to the consumer's use before discovering the defect.

    • + What if I change my mind and no longer want the product?

      The seller can refuse to replace a product or refund the amount paid if the request is by virtue of the consumer’s change of mind or mistake and not due to product imperfection or defect. However, some businesses implement an internal policy that allows refunds only as an act of goodwill to consumers.

  • No Return No Exchange Policy

    • + What is No Return No Exchange Policy?

      No Return, No Exchange is not enforced by the DTI. It is prohibited under the Consumer Act of the Philippines or RA 7394. According to the law, it is prohibited to put the words “No Return, No Exchange” on receipts, in any area of ​the store, or on any document proving the sale.

    • + Why is it prohibited to include a “No Return No Exchange” policy on receipts or any document to prove the sale?

      This is because it violates the consumer's right to have the product repaired, replaced, or refunded if the purchased product is or has been defective or damaged since purchase. If you remember, these are our 3Rs – repair, replace, refund.

    • + Can I ask for a refund if I changed my mind and do not want the product anymore?

      No. The seller can refuse to replace a product or refund the amount paid if the request is by virtue of the consumer’s change of mind or mistake and not due to product imperfection or defect. However, some businesses implement an internal policy that allows refunds only as an act of goodwill to consumers.

      Aside from a change in mind, please referee to the Warranty section for other situations.

    • + Can a store exercise only a policy of exchange but not refund?

      Consumers are entitled to either an exchage or refund, as long as there is a defect in the quality of goods or imperfection in the service.

    • + Is there a time limit within which a buyer may return defective products?

      There is no hard-and-fast rule on the period within which a customer may return the products he purchased. A rule of reason should, however, be observed, taking into consideration the nature of the item purchased and the express / implied warranties mandated by law, i.e. Consumer Act and the New Civil Code of the Philippines.

    • + Can a buyer return defective goods without the official receipt?

      The Official Receipt is the best proof of purchase. However, he/she may still demand replacement or refund if he/she can prove that a defective item was bought from a certain store.

    • Senior Citizen and Person with Disability Discount

    • + Who is a Senior Citizen?

      A Senior Citizen or elderly is any Filipino citizen who is a resident of the Philippines, and who is sixty (60) years old or above. It may apply to senior citizens with “dual citizenship” status, provided they prove their Filipino citizenship and have at least six (6) months residency in the Philippines. [RA9994]

    • + Who is a Person with Disability?

      A Person With Disability is a person who has a long-term physical, mental, intellectual or sensory impairment which in interaction with various barriers may hinder their full and effective participation in society on an equal basis with others. [IRR of RA 10754]

    • + What are the laws governing Senior Citizen Discount and Person with Disability Discount?

      The law governing Senior Citizens discount is Republic Act No. 9994 (RA 9994), or the Expanded Senior Citizens Act of 2010, amending RA Nos.7432 and 9257, while the law governing the rights and privileged for Persons With Disability is RA 10754, or An Act Expanding the Benefits and Privileges of Persons with Disability, amending RA Nos. 7277, 9442 and 10070.

    • + What are the products and services that a senior citizen/person with disability is entitled to a discount?

      1. 20% discount and value-added tax (VAT) exemption on the purchase of:
        • Medical-related privileges:
          • Medicine and drugs purchases
          • Essential medical supplies, accessories and equipment
          • Medical and dental services
          • Professional fees of license professional health providing home health care services
          • Professional fees of attending physicians
        • Domestic transportation privileges
        • Fare for public land, domestic air, and sea travel
        • Hotels, restaurants, recreational centers, and places of leisure, and recreation center
        • Admission fees charged by theaters, cinema houses and concert halls, circuses, leisure and amusement
        • Funeral and Burial Services
      2. 5% special discount of the regular retail price, without exemption from the value-added tax (VAT), of basic necessities and prime commodities (BNPCs), listed in Section 2 (a) and (b) of the Joint Administrative Order (JAO) No. 24-02, Series of 2024 or the “2024 Revised Rules on Granting Special Discounts to Senior Citizens and Persons with Disability on Purchase of Basic Necessities and Prime Commodities (BNPCs)

    • + What are Basic Necessities and Prime Commodities (BNPCs)?

      1. Basic necessities are goods vital to the needs of consumers for their sustenance and existence in times of any cases provided in RA7581 or the Price Act such as:
        • Medical-related privileges:
        • All kinds and variants of rice (except imported specialty rice)
        • All kinds and variants of corn
        • All kinds of locally produced bread (cakes and pastries not included)
        • All locally produced fresh, dried, and canned fish, and other marine products including those live, chilled, frozen, and poultry meat (except premium grade)
        • All locally produced fresh pork, beef, and poultry meat (except premium grade)
        • All fresh chicken and duck eggs
        • Locally manufactured potable water in bottles and containers regardless of size
        • Fresh and processed milk (excluding foods for special medical purposes)
        • Locally produced fresh vegetables including root crops
        • All kinds of fresh fruits
        • Locally manufactured instant noodles
        • Locally produced coffee (only whole beans, ground beans, instant up to three (3) main ingredients)
        • All kinds of sugar (except artificial sweeteners)
        • Cooking oil (only coconut oil, palm oil, soybean oil, canola oil, and vegetable oil)
        • Iodized salt
        • Locally manufactured laundry and detergent soap
        • Locally produced firewood
        • Locally manufactured charcoal
        • All kinds of candles (except decorative and scented)
        • Household liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) with a net content of not more than eleven (11) kilograms only once every five (50 months) bought from LPG dealers
        • Kerosene, not more than two liters per month.
      2. Prime commodities are goods NOT considered as basic necessities but are essential to consumers in times of any cases provided in RA7581 such as:
        • All kinds of flour
        • All locally manufactured dried, processed and canned pork, beef, and poultry meat
        • All kinds of locally manufactured dairy products not falling under Section 1(a)(8) of this Order
        • All kinds of onions and garlic
        • Locally manufactured vinegar, patis, and soy sauce (except spiced)
        • All kinds of toilet or bath soap
        • Fertilizer
        • Pesticides
        • Herbicides
        • Poultry feeds, livestock feeds, and fishery feeds
        • Veterinary products
        • Pad paper, school supplies
        • Nipa shingle
        • Sawali
        • Cement, clinker, GI sheets
        • Hollow blocks
        • Plywood
        • Plyboard
        • Construction nails
        • Batteries (single-use household batteries)
        • Electrical supplies and light bulbs
        • Steel wires

    • + Is there a purchase limit on the purchase of BNPCs by a senior citizen, or person with disability subject to a special discount?

      Yes, the senior citizen and the person with disability has a purchase limit on BNPCs per week subject to a 5% special discount.

      The total maximum amount of offline and online purchases of BNPCs for senior citizens and persons with disability in a week is Two Thousand Five Hundred Pesos (PHP2,500.00) commensurate to his/her personal and exclusive consumption or enjoyment, or a total special discount of One Hundred Twenty-Five Pesos (PHP125.00) per calendar week, without carryover of the unused discount.

      Provided that if the full amount of PHP2,500.00 shall be utilized, such shall be spent on at least four (4) kinds of items listed under Section 2(a) and (b) of JAO 24-02.

    • + What are the valid identification documents (ID) to be presented to avail of the senior citizen or person with a disability discount?

      1. The senior citizen, or his/her duly authorized representative, may present as proof of eligibility any of the following:
        • OSCA-issued SC ID card
        • ANY government-issued identification document indicating the SC age or birthdate as well as his/her Filipino Citizenship
        • Philippine Passport
        • Driver’s License
        • Voter’s ID
        • SSS/GSIS ID
        • PRC Card
        • Postal ID
      2. The person with disability, or his/her duly authorized representative, may present as proof of eligibility any of the following:
        • ID card issued by PDAO or the C/MSWDO
        • Passport
        • ID issued by NCDA
        • Any government issued ID as proof of entitlement

  • Contracts

    • + What is Contract?

      According to the Civil Code of the Philippines, Title II, Chapter 1, Article 1305: A contract is a meeting of minds between two persons whereby one binds himself, with respect to the other, to give something or to render some service

      There is no contract unless the following requisites concur:

      (1) Consent of the contracting parties;

      (2) Object (or service) which is the subject matter of the contract;

      (3) Cause of the obligation which is established.(1261)

    • + What can DTI do with regards to contracts?

      Your transaction with these entities are governed by a contract and is beyond the authority of DTI. In this regard, the terms and conditions thereof, particularly the provisions on payments, penalties, and termination constitute a binding agreement, provided they are not contrary to law, morals, good customs, public order, or public policy.

      We advise you to check the terms of the contract, particularly on cancellation, termination, and refund to guide you on your matters, as well as to reach out to a representative from the entity.

  • Deceptive, Unfair, and Unconscionable Sales Act

    • + What are these Deceptive, unfair and unconscionable sales acts and practices?

      According to the Consumer Act of the Philippines (RA 7394), deceptive, unfair and unconscionable sales acts and practices or what we call deceptive practices by a seller or supplier are illegal whether they occur before, during or after a transaction.

    • + How can a consumer know if an activity is deceptive or fraudulent?

      An act is considered deceptive when a producer, manufacturer, supplier, or seller induces a consumer to enter into a transaction for the sale or rental of any consumer product or service, by concealing material information, misrepresenting, or fraudulent manipulation.

    • + What if the seller refuses to refund or does not accept the product return?

      According to the Civil Code of the Philippines, if the seller breaches the warranty, the buyer or consumer may cancel the contract of sale and refuse to accept the product. In case the product has already been received, they may be returned and the consumer's payment will be refunded.


      You may also report to the DTI Provincial or Regional Office in your area or on our website: https://consumercare.dti.gov.ph/#/home

  • Price Tag

    • + What is Price Tag?

      According to the Consumer Act of the Philippines, a price tag is a label that states the price of a product to provide consumers with sufficient information and guidance to compare the prices of products.

    • + Where should the price tag be placed?

      According to DTI Department Administrative Order No. 09, series of 2002, the price tag must be properly placed on the shelf or rack where the product in question is displayed. It must be secured to minimize misplacement, tampering, or loss.

    • + What if the price on the shelf tag is different from the counter or cashier?

      The stores must ensure that the price on the shelf, on the tag, or on the checker is the same price at the counter or at the cashier.

  • E-Commerce

    E-commerce is a service provided by third party entities. Registering for their website and purchasing or selling an item is considered as entering a contract with the entity. Please refer to the “Contract” section for more information.

    • + Seller fails to comply with delivery date

      If the seller induced you to buy the item through promises of guaranteed delivery and the same was not fulfilled, the seller may be liable for a deceptive sales act.

      As to whether you may demand expedited delivery, the same shall be the subject of an agreement between you and the seller.

  • Price Freeze

    • + What is Price Freeze?

      Republic Act (RA) No. 7581 or what we call the Price Act states that a price freeze is automatically implemented in areas that have been declared under a state of emergency, calamity, disaster, rebellion, or war, or that have been placed under martial law, or that have been suspended what we call the privilege of writ of habeas corpus. Note, the DTI does not declare a price freeze.

    • + How long is the price freeze in effect?

      The price freeze is in effect for a maximum of 60 days for products that are considered basic necessities and 15 days for household LPG and liquid fuel. It may be shortened if the conditions that led to the implementation of the price freeze are lifted earlier. It may also lifted if the President of the Philippines lifts it.

    • + All products are covered by the price freeze?

      The price freeze covers products that fall under the category of “basic necessities.” This means that stores cannot increase the prices of “basic necessities” such as sardines, milk, coffee, instant noodles, bread, candles, salt, including rice, pork, chicken and beef, fish, vegetables under the jurisdiction of the Department of Agriculture, and essential medicines as prescribed by the Department of Health.

    • + Why is a price freeze necessary?

      This is implemented to prevent exploitation during times of calamity or unrest.

    • + In case of violation of this law, what could be the penalties for businesses or stores?

      Whoever violates this law shall be punished by imprisonment of not less than one (1) year nor more than ten (10) years and a fine of not less than five thousand pesos (P5,000) nor more than one million pesos (P1,000,000), or both, at the discretion of the court.
